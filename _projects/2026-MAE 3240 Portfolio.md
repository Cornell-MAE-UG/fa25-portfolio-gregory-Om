I found that Problem set 2 was the most valuable throughout my time with this heat transfer class. This homework stood out to me because of its focus on taking the heat transfer theory we learned in class and applying it directly to real-life problems where this type of analysis is useful. This is particularly the case for question 3 with laser additive manufacturing. What made this question especially impactful was how it demonstrated that the heat equation can be used to solve for heat fluxes simply by knowing the temperature distribution, something we had never been explicitly introduced to before. Before, we always started with known conditions and solved forward; this problem flipped that thinking and showed that working backwards from a temperature profile is actually rather practical. I found this to be one of the most valuable lessons in engineering problem solving: understanding what problems CAN be solved based on the information available (this was especially the case when I was at my Co-op). It reflects how real engineering analysis often works, where you are given incomplete or indirect information and must use fundamental principles to draw conclusions. Granted, in this case we were given everything that was needed, but it shows how this is when we use specific measuring devices specifically for problem solving.

Furthermore, I also wanted to talk about the first question involving the electric water heater. Not only is this a practical use case for, yet again, applying the theory in a real-life case, this problem in particular built some useful habits for me when solving more problems in the future. The habit of defining my system boundaries and applying conservation of heat principles allowed me to break this problem up into something more digestible. From a more practical perspective, this question also puts the scale in which practical insulation is used and the magnitude of their impact when regulating temperature. I find that understanding this scale (and the variables that go with thermal management), show how powerful a REALLY good design can be even when something as simple as adding foam around the sides makes such a large difference in energy saving.
